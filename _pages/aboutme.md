Ok back to my self intro! I'm a linguistics student and I do linguistics research. In my undergraduate years, I started off being interested in theoretical syntax and Chinese linguistics (both Modern Chinese and Ancient Chinese) so I initially majored in Chinese Language and Literature in CUHK. My interest shifted after taking some courses, reading more about linguistics, and talking with some friends. As my interests in interdisciplinary approaches to linguistics had grown, eventually I transferred to the Department of Linguistics and Modern Languages, where I did a corpus study on bilingual acquisition under the excellent supervision and guidance of Prof. Virginia Yip and Prof. Stephen Matthews. (The study got published in the International Journal of Bilingualism lately btw.) Currently I'd say that my research interests are psycholinguistics and language acquisition.

My research interests are now centred around the questions of how speakers process language in real-time and how this plays a role in language acquisition. I believe figuring out how human acquires language is one of the most difficult and most important problems yet to be solved currently. My current view of the problem is kinda non-Chomskyan: I do think innate constraints on the grammar of human language exist, but I am not sure if these innate constraints are unique to language acquisition (or if they are really exclusive to human beings fundamentally). It is much more convincing to me if these innate constraints are a part of our general cognitive abilities/derived from general cognitive principles, such that they might be found in not only language acquisition, but also some other learning behaviours of ours. It is also more convincing to me if language is so-called human-exclusive simply because human's cognitive abilities are more powerful than other animals thanks to a more complicated neural network (such that those innate constraints can be derived from our powerful cognitive abilities), rather than because human somehow suddenly developed an independent "language faculty" in our brain, which makes less sense when you think of evolution.
